\section{Process-Level Integration}\label{sec:process-implications}
For a loaded \MEA solvent to be useful in process models, the thermodynamic description must transfer cleanly to absorber/stripper calculations without loss of electrolyte and chemical-equilibrium physics.  The full-ionic ePC-SAFT formulation developed here is structured for that transfer.  At each unit stage, the property package accepts temperature, pressure, total apparent composition, and loading; solves charge-balanced liquid speciation; evaluates activity and fugacity quantities; and returns the \COtwo equilibrium pressure or phase-equilibrium residual required by the process solver.

This coupling matters most in rich-solvent and high-temperature regions.  In an absorber, low \COtwo partial pressures require accurate lean-solvent activity and speciation predictions because small pressure errors alter the driving force for mass transfer.  In a stripper, high temperature and high loading move the system into a region where \COtwo pressure increases steeply with loading; an error in the rich-end pressure curve can therefore affect predicted reboiler duty, cyclic capacity, and solvent circulation rate.  A model that fits only the VLE curve but gives an incorrect carbamate/bicarbonate distribution can still give misleading heat-release and solvent-capacity trends.

The process interface should expose three outputs: the \COtwo equilibrium partial pressure, the liquid true-species composition, and activity/fugacity diagnostics used to verify thermodynamic consistency.  These outputs are sufficient for a rate-based absorber model, an equilibrium-stage stripper calculation, or a reduced solvent-cycle analysis.

A compact set of process-relevant metrics follows from these outputs: \COtwo removal, rich loading, lean loading, equilibrium approach along the column, and sensitivity of solvent circulation and reboiler duty to the fitted electrolyte parameters.  A numerical absorber/stripper case study is outside the scope of this manuscript and is identified as follow-on work.
