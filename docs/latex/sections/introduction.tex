\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
Post-combustion \COtwo capture using aqueous alkanolamines is a mature route for separating \COtwo from dilute combustion gas streams.  Monoethanolamine (\MEA) is a standard reference solvent because it has a long record of use and a broad experimental literature.  Its thermodynamic representation remains challenging because absorbed \COtwo does not behave as a neutral dissolved gas alone.  Instead, it reacts with water and amine to form a liquid mixture containing molecular species, protonated amine, carbamate, bicarbonate, carbonate, hydronium, and hydroxide.  The equilibrium gas pressure is therefore coupled to both phase equilibrium and chemical speciation.

In absorber and stripper calculations, equilibrium \COtwo partial pressure controls the gas-phase driving force used by rate-based or equilibrium-stage models.  Liquid speciation constrains the chemically available amine, the amount of carbamate formed, and the consistency of rich and lean loading calculations.  A pressure-only fit can therefore reproduce a pressure curve while hiding an implausible distribution of \MEA, \MEAH, \MEACOO, \HCOthree, and \COthree species.  For that reason, this work evaluates pressure and speciation together.

\subsection{MEA and reactive CO2 absorption}
Loaded aqueous \MEA is a reactive electrolyte solution, not a simple physical-solubility system.  The apparent variables used in many data sets, such as unloaded amine mass fraction and \COtwo loading, must be reconciled with a true-species liquid composition before an equation of state can evaluate fugacities or activity coefficients.  The experimental basis used here combines VLE measurements of \COtwo partial pressure with NMR measurements of liquid-phase species.  These data families are complementary: pressure data constrain the phase-equilibrium response, whereas speciation data constrain whether the liquid composition remains chemically plausible.

\subsection{Thermodynamic modeling of MEA--CO2--H2O}
Engineering models for \COtwo-loaded amines have historically relied on apparent-species correlations or activity-coefficient models.  Kent--Eisenberg and related apparent-equilibrium approaches provide compact correlations but have limited transferability outside their fitted temperature, loading, and solvent-composition windows.  Electrolyte-NRTL and related excess-Gibbs-energy models improved the representation of ionic liquid nonideality and are widely used in process simulators for \MEA--\COtwo--\HtwoO systems\cite{Zhang2011,Morgan2017b,Mouhoubi2020,Hilliard2008}.  These models are valuable for process calculations, but their reliability depends strongly on fitted interaction parameters and on the species convention used for regression.

The \MEA literature contains enough pressure and speciation measurements to test a more molecularly explicit description.  VLE measurements provide \COtwo partial pressures across temperature, loading, and amine-composition conditions\cite{Jou1995,Hilliard2008,Mamun2005,Xu2011,Aronu2011}.  NMR measurements provide independent liquid-phase speciation constraints for \MEA, \MEAH, \MEACOO, \HCOthree, and carbonate-containing species\cite{Jakobsen2005,Bottinger2008,Matin2012}.  A thermodynamic model that is intended to support process calculations should therefore report both gas-phase pressure residuals and liquid-speciation residuals.

\subsection{SAFT and PC-SAFT models for alkanolamines}
SAFT-type equations of state provide an alternative to purely excess-Gibbs-energy descriptions because molecular size, dispersion, chain formation, and association are represented in the Helmholtz-energy model.  The PC-SAFT equation of Gross and Sadowski introduced a perturbation theory for chain molecules\cite{Gross2001}, and its associating extension provides the site-based hydrogen-bonding contribution needed for water and alkanolamines\cite{Gross2002}.  PC-SAFT has been applied directly to aqueous ethanolamine systems and acid-gas solubility, including pure alkanolamine property regression and VLE prediction for aqueous amines\cite{Nasrifar2010}.  Baygi and Pahlavanzadeh applied PC-SAFT to \COtwo solubility in aqueous \MEA, using a chemical-equilibrium step to reconstruct liquid species and a neutral PC-SAFT pressure calculation as the phase-equilibrium model\cite{Baygi2015}.  Other SAFT variants have also been used for loaded \MEA, including transferable SAFT-VR models that represent chemisorption stoichiometry through effective association sites\cite{MacDowell2010}.  More recent SAFT comparisons for alkanolamines continue to show that EOS-based models can describe pure, binary, and ternary amine properties with physically interpretable parameters\cite{Pakravesh2025a}.

These studies establish the relevance of SAFT-family models for amine thermodynamics, but most PC-SAFT treatments of loaded \MEA either use an apparent chemistry representation or handle the reactive liquid composition outside the electrolyte EOS.  Consequently, the liquid nonideality of ionic species is not fully coupled to the reaction equilibria through ePC-SAFT activities.

\subsection{ePC-SAFT and advanced Born electrostatics}
Electrolyte PC-SAFT (ePC-SAFT) extends PC-SAFT to charged species by adding an electrolyte contribution to the residual Helmholtz energy.  The original aqueous-electrolyte formulation introduced Debye--Huckel electrostatic effects into the PC-SAFT framework and demonstrated correlations for aqueous salt densities and vapor pressures\cite{Cameretti2005}.  Uyan et al. applied ePC-SAFT to aqueous MDEA--\COtwo solubility with explicit ionic species and activity-coupled chemical equilibrium, treating the calculation as predictive when no binary parameters were adjusted to the ternary acid-gas data\cite{Uyan2015}.  Wangler et al. extended that amine-solubility strategy by fitting selected binary interactions to independent osmotic-coefficient data and validating the resulting model against acid-gas solubility, thereby distinguishing literature-collected pure-component parameters, independently regressed binary parameters, and final VLE validation data\cite{Wangler2018}.

Later ePC-SAFT developments addressed non-aqueous and mixed-solvent electrolytes, where ion solvation and composition-dependent permittivity become central.  The ePC-SAFT advanced formulation of Buelow, Ascani, and Held incorporated a concentration-dependent dielectric constant into both the Born contribution and Debye--Huckel term\cite{Bulow2020}; the second part extended the framework to salt solubility and ion pairing\cite{Bulow2021}.  Subsequent work used ePC-SAFT advanced to predict \COtwo solubility in aqueous and organic electrolyte solutions, including systems with carbonate chemistry\cite{Schick2023}.  More recent studies further refined permittivity modeling and modified Born terms in electrolyte PC-SAFT\cite{Rueben2024,Figiel2025}.

The loaded \MEA system is a natural target for this model class.  It contains associating neutral molecules, molecular \COtwo, large solvent-composition changes, and multiple ionic products of acid--base chemistry.  A true-species ePC-SAFT model can therefore connect phase equilibrium, reaction equilibrium, ionic activity coefficients, and solvation effects in one thermodynamic framework.

\subsection{Contribution of this work}
This work constructs and evaluates a true-species activity-based ePC-SAFT description of aqueous \MEA loaded with \COtwo.  It is not an empirical pressure-only fit.  We assemble literature VLE and speciation records on a common loading basis, define a nine-species basis containing \COtwo, \MEA, \HtwoO, \MEAH, \MEACOO, \HCOthree, \COthree, \HthreeO, and \Hydroxide, and use an ideal Smith--Missen-style baseline to verify reaction and balance conventions before introducing ePC-SAFT activities.  We then evaluate the fixed activity-based parameter set against pressure and speciation records using SSM+DS Born and concentration-dependent dielectric terms, and we delimit the result as a residual-qualified Phase 1 and Phase 2 evaluation rather than a finalized global regression.
