\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
Post-combustion carbon capture (PCC) is a vital technology for mitigating
$\COtwo$ emissions, with amine-based absorption using monoethanolamine (\MEA)
remaining one of the most established routes for \COtwo removal from dilute flue
gas streams.  Understanding the thermodynamics of the \MEA--\HtwoO--\COtwo
system is essential for designing efficient absorption and desorption processes.
Traditional models, including electrolyte NRTL, extended UNIQUAC, and empirical
correlations, have long been used to correlate vapor-liquid equilibrium (VLE),
reactive speciation, and other properties of these mixtures.  In recent years,
molecular-based equations of state, including the statistical associating fluid
theory (SAFT) family and its PC-SAFT and ePC-SAFT variants, have become
attractive alternatives for complex associating and electrolyte systems.  These
equations of state offer a more physical parameterization than purely empirical
models while retaining the computational efficiency required for process
simulation.

\subsection{Prior Work on MEA Thermodynamics, VLE, and Solubility}
Monoethanolamine has long been the benchmark solvent for post-combustion
\ce{CO2} capture due to its high reactivity with acidic gases and relatively low
cost.  Accurate representation of its thermodynamics, including VLE, heats of
absorption, and speciation, is therefore essential for predictive process
modeling.  Early empirical and activity coefficient models such as
Kent-Eisenberg and Deshmukh-Mather provided approximate frameworks but suffered
from limited extrapolation capability~\cite{Liu1999,Song2009,Akula2023a}.  The
electrolyte nonrandom two-liquid (eNRTL) model became a standard in commercial
simulators and has been refined in several studies for
\MEA--\ce{CO2}--\ce{H2O} systems~\cite{Zhang2011,Morgan2017b,Mouhoubi2020,Hilliard2008}.
Experimental studies have further provided high-quality solubility and
calorimetric data for MEA solutions, establishing reliable baselines for model
development and validation~\cite{Zhu2022,Kim2021}.  Despite this progress,
activity coefficient models typically require large numbers of fitted binary
parameters and have limited transferability outside the conditions used for
parameter estimation.

\subsection{Prior Applications of PC-SAFT and ePC-SAFT to Amine Systems}
Equations of state (EOS), and in particular the SAFT family, provide a more
molecularly grounded alternative to excess Gibbs energy approaches.  The
perturbed-chain SAFT (PC-SAFT) equation of state has been widely used to model
associating systems, including alkanolamine solutions.  Applications include
the work of Nasrifar and co-workers on MEA and other
ethanolamines~\cite{Nasrifar2010}, as well as extensions to solubility and
calorimetric data for \MEA--\ce{CO2}--\ce{H2O}~\cite{Baygi2015,Najafloo2018}.
More recent efforts have expanded SAFT-based modeling to multiple alkanolamines
and mixtures, with PC-SAFT, SAFT-HR, and P$\rho$T-SAFT-HR formulations evaluated
across pure, binary, and ternary systems~\cite{Pakravesh2025}.  These studies
highlight the promise of SAFT-type EOSs for reducing reliance on empirical
fitting while retaining predictive capability for VLE, enthalpy, and density
properties.

The electrolyte extension, ePC-SAFT, incorporates explicit treatment of charged
species, enabling physically consistent modeling of aqueous electrolyte
solutions.  ePC-SAFT has demonstrated success in systems containing ionic
liquids and mixed solvents~\cite{Sun2019,Bulow2021}.  Thus, while PC-SAFT has
shown predictive capability for \ce{CO2}-loaded alkanolamines, the explicit
electrolyte treatment of ePC-SAFT offers an attractive pathway to capture the
speciation and solvation effects inherent in loaded MEA systems.

\subsection{Research Gap and Objective}
Although MEA has been extensively studied using empirical, activity
coefficient, and PC-SAFT approaches, there remains a gap in the systematic
application of the ePC-SAFT framework to the
\MEA--\ce{CO2}--\ce{H2O} system.  Existing PC-SAFT studies provide valuable
benchmarks, but they do not explicitly represent electrolyte contributions,
limiting their ability to capture speciation and solvation thermodynamics at
high loadings.  Conversely, electrolyte activity coefficient models such as
eNRTL provide engineering utility but require extensive parameterization and
offer limited predictive transferability.

The objective of this work is therefore to build a thermodynamic modeling
workflow for the MEA system using ePC-SAFT.  The present manuscript organizes
the experimental VLE and speciation data, defines a charge-balanced
true-species liquid basis, reports baseline chemistry and pressure diagnostics,
and establishes the parameter-regression structure needed for the final
ePC-SAFT model.  Once the fitted parameters are finalized, the resulting model
will provide the thermodynamic foundation for rigorous process simulation of
amine-based \ce{CO2} capture.
