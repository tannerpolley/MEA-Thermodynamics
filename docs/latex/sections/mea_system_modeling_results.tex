\section{MEA System Modeling Results}\label{sec:mea-system-modeling-results}

This section summarizes the current modeling workflow for the aqueous
\MEA--\COtwo--\HtwoO system.  The purpose at this stage is to define the data
basis, chemical speciation model, baseline pressure calculation, and regression
path for a true-species electrolyte PC-SAFT representation.  The workflow uses
the experimental VLE and speciation data collected in the project repository and
maps those measurements onto a nine-species reactive liquid basis suitable for
ePC-SAFT.

\subsection{Experimental Data Basis}

The raw data used for the MEA system are separated into vapor-liquid equilibrium
data and chemical speciation data.  The VLE data contain unloaded MEA
mass fraction, temperature, \COtwo loading, and measured \COtwo partial pressure.
The assembled VLE table contains measurements from Aronu et al., Hilliard,
Jou et al., Mamun et al., and Xu et al.~\cite{Aronu2011,Hilliard2008,Jou1995,Mamun2005,Xu2011}.
The speciation table contains NMR-derived true-species mole fractions for
aqueous loaded MEA, including measurements from Boettinger et al., Jakobsen
et al., and Matin et al.~\cite{Bottinger2008,Jakobsen2005,Matin2012}.  These
two data families are treated as complementary: VLE data constrain the gas-phase
\COtwo pressure response, while speciation data constrain the distribution of
neutral MEA, protonated MEA, carbamate, bicarbonate, and carbonate in the liquid.

\subsection{Reactive Species Basis}

The apparent liquid feed is defined from the unloaded MEA mass fraction
$w_{\MEA}$ and the \COtwo loading $\alpha$, where $\alpha$ is the number of
moles of absorbed \COtwo per mole of total MEA.  For an unloaded solution basis,
the apparent MEA mole fraction is computed from molecular-weight conversion as
\begin{equation}
    x_{\MEA,0}
    =
    \frac{w_{\MEA}}
    {M_{\MEA}/M_{\HtwoO} + w_{\MEA}\left(1-M_{\MEA}/M_{\HtwoO}\right)} ,
    \label{eq:mea-unloaded-mole-fraction}
\end{equation}
with $x_{\HtwoO,0}=1-x_{\MEA,0}$.  The apparent charged solution is then formed
by adding absorbed \COtwo according to
\begin{equation}
    n_{\COtwo,0} = \alpha n_{\MEA,0},
    \qquad
    x_{i,0} = \frac{n_{i,0}}{n_{\COtwo,0}+n_{\MEA,0}+n_{\HtwoO,0}} .
    \label{eq:mea-apparent-feed}
\end{equation}
This apparent ternary state is converted into a true-species liquid composition.
The current diagnostic model uses the nine-species set
\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{x}
    =
    \left(
    x_{\COtwo},
    x_{\MEA},
    x_{\HtwoO},
    x_{\MEAH},
    x_{\MEACOO},
    x_{\HCOthree},
    x_{\ce{CO3^2-}},
    x_{\ce{H3O+}},
    x_{\ce{OH-}}
    \right).
    \label{eq:mea-nine-species-vector}
\end{equation}
This species basis makes the electrolyte character of the loaded solvent
explicit and is therefore the basis used for the ePC-SAFT parameter folder.

\subsection{Chemical Equilibrium Model}

The true-species calculation is formulated as a simultaneous reaction-equilibrium
and conservation problem.  For each independent reaction $r$, the equilibrium
condition is written in mole-fraction form as
\begin{equation}
    \sum_i \nu_{r i}\ln x_i = \ln K_r(T),
    \label{eq:mea-log-equilibrium}
\end{equation}
where $\nu_{ri}$ is the stoichiometric coefficient of species $i$ in reaction
$r$.  The temperature dependence of each equilibrium constant follows the
correlation form
\begin{equation}
    \ln K_r(T) = a_r + \frac{b_r}{T} + c_r \ln T + d_r T.
    \label{eq:mea-equilibrium-temperature}
\end{equation}
The current nine-species solver includes water autoionization, bicarbonate and
carbonate equilibria, carbamate hydrolysis, and MEA protonation/carbamate
formation.  The equations are solved with element and charge balances:
\begin{align}
    x_{\COtwo,0} &= x_{\COtwo}+x_{\MEACOO}+x_{\HCOthree}+x_{\ce{CO3^2-}}, \\
    x_{\MEA,0} &= x_{\MEA}+x_{\MEAH}+x_{\MEACOO}, \\
    x_{\HtwoO,0} &= x_{\HtwoO}+x_{\HCOthree}+x_{\ce{CO3^2-}}+x_{\ce{H3O+}}+x_{\ce{OH-}}, \\
    x_{\MEAH}+x_{\ce{H3O+}} &= x_{\MEACOO}+x_{\HCOthree}+2x_{\ce{CO3^2-}}+x_{\ce{OH-}}.
    \label{eq:mea-nine-species-balances}
\end{align}
This model is intentionally more detailed than the older six-species baseline.
The six-species model is useful as a continuity check because it preserves the
legacy chemistry and pressure path, while the nine-species model is the more
natural bridge into ePC-SAFT because it carries the ions required by the
electrolyte term.

The all-species sweep currently solves all 101 diagnostic speciation states
without failed loadings.  This is an important practical result because it
allows the ePC-SAFT calculation to be driven from a continuous, charge-balanced
composition path rather than from isolated measured points.  The present
speciation calculation should still be interpreted as a diagnostic chemistry
model.  In the final parameter-estimation workflow, these speciation states are
expected to become either direct fitting targets or fixed liquid-state inputs
for early-stage VLE parameter regression.

\subsection{Baseline Pressure Model}

The pressure baseline is used to check that the chemistry path produces plausible
\COtwo pressure trends before the ePC-SAFT regression is applied.  In the legacy
pressure calculation, the liquid composition is collapsed from the reactive
species basis back to an apparent \COtwo--\MEA--\HtwoO composition and evaluated
with a PC-SAFT pressure model.  The resulting residuals are reported on a
logarithmic scale,
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon_P =
    \log_{10}\left(\frac{P_{\COtwo}^{\mathrm{model}}}
    {P_{\COtwo}^{\mathrm{exp}}}\right),
    \label{eq:mea-pressure-residual}
\end{equation}
because the measured \COtwo partial pressures span several orders of magnitude.
The objective of this baseline is not to replace the ePC-SAFT model, but to
verify that the raw data, loading convention, and species reconstruction are
internally consistent.

The pressure figures show the expected steep increase in \COtwo partial pressure
with loading.  This behavior is important for carbon capture process simulation:
small changes in loading near the rich end of the solvent cycle can correspond
to large changes in equilibrium pressure and therefore large changes in absorber
or stripper driving force.  The diagnostic pressure results also show why
parameter regression is required.  The chemistry model gives a stable
composition path, but an electrolyte equation of state must still be calibrated
against both pressure and speciation information before it can be used as a
predictive thermodynamic engine.

\subsection{ePC-SAFT Parameterization Workflow}

The working ePC-SAFT dataset represents the liquid using the nine species in
\cref{eq:mea-nine-species-vector}.  Neutral species retain PC-SAFT segment,
diameter, dispersion, and association parameters, while charged species require
ion diameter, dispersion, charge, and Born-radius parameters.  The working
parameter table currently includes explicit entries for \COtwo, \MEA, \HtwoO,
\MEAH, \MEACOO, \HCOthree, \ce{CO3^2-}, \ce{H3O+}, and \ce{OH-}.  The current
regression focus is on the species most directly controlled by the MEA reaction
chemistry: \MEA, \MEAH, and \MEACOO.

Binary interaction parameters are included for the pairs most likely to control
the VLE response in the preliminary model.  The current parameter set contains nonzero
$k_{ij}$ values for \COtwo--\MEA, \COtwo--\MEAH, \COtwo--\MEACOO, and
\MEA--\HtwoO.  Hydrogen-bonding cross-interaction parameters are not yet active
in the working table.  This produces a deliberately simple first regression
surface: tune the dominant pure-ion and binary-interaction degrees of freedom
before adding additional cross association or composition-dependent dielectric
terms.

The ePC-SAFT regression target can be expressed as a weighted least-squares
problem over VLE and speciation records:
\begin{equation}
    \Phi(\boldsymbol{\theta}) =
    \sum_{j\in\mathcal{D}_{P}}
    w_{P,j}
    \left[
    \log_{10}P_{\COtwo,j}^{\mathrm{model}}(\boldsymbol{\theta})
    -
    \log_{10}P_{\COtwo,j}^{\mathrm{exp}}
    \right]^2
    +
    \sum_{j\in\mathcal{D}_{x}}
    \sum_{i\in\mathcal{S}_{x}}
    w_{x,ij}
    \left[
    x_{ij}^{\mathrm{model}}(\boldsymbol{\theta})
    -
    x_{ij}^{\mathrm{exp}}
    \right]^2 ,
    \label{eq:mea-regression-objective}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is the vector of adjustable ePC-SAFT parameters,
$\mathcal{D}_{P}$ is the VLE data set, $\mathcal{D}_{x}$ is the speciation data
set, and $\mathcal{S}_{x}$ is the set of measured liquid species.  The pressure
term is written in logarithmic form to avoid over-weighting high-pressure rich
loading data.  The speciation term provides an independent constraint on the
chemical distribution, preventing the regression from matching pressure through
unphysical species partitioning.

For the first complete ePC-SAFT workflow, the practical regression sequence is:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Build a charge-balanced liquid composition path from the raw
    speciation data and the nine-species reaction model.
    \item Fit the adjustable \MEA, \MEAH, and \MEACOO pure-component parameters
    against the speciation-informed liquid states.
    \item Fit the dominant binary interaction parameters against the VLE partial
    pressures, beginning with \COtwo--\MEA, \COtwo--\MEAH, \COtwo--\MEACOO, and
    \MEA--\HtwoO.
    \item Recompute the VLE and speciation figures using the fitted parameter
    folder and compare the residuals in \cref{eq:mea-pressure-residual} and
    \cref{eq:mea-regression-objective}.
\end{enumerate}
This ordering separates numerical stability from thermodynamic calibration.
The current chemistry and pressure plots demonstrate that the raw MEA data can
be converted into a continuous true-species path.  The next modeling task is to
use that path as the fitting basis for the electrolyte equation of state rather
than treating the existing diagnostic pressure model as the final result.

\subsection{Current Interpretation and Next Modeling Step}

The present results establish three points.  First, the raw VLE and speciation
data have been organized into a consistent loading and species convention.
Second, the nine-species chemistry model is sufficiently robust for a continuous
30 wt\% MEA loading sweep, which is necessary for process-simulation use.
Third, the pressure diagnostics reproduce the correct qualitative trend but
still require ePC-SAFT regression before the model can be considered predictive.

The final results section should replace the diagnostic pressure comparison with
a fitted ePC-SAFT comparison.  The expected final evidence set
should include: (i) the fitted pure and binary parameters, (ii) parity or
residual plots for \COtwo pressure, (iii) overlays of measured and predicted
liquid speciation, and (iv) a short uncertainty or sensitivity discussion
showing which parameters control pressure and which control speciation.  Section
4 can then use the completed thermodynamic model as the property package for
the absorption-column simulation.
