\section{Introduction}\label{sec:introduction}
Post-combustion \COtwo capture using aqueous alkanolamines is one of the most mature routes for reducing emissions from dilute combustion gas streams.  Monoethanolamine (\MEA) is the standard reference solvent because it combines rapid reaction kinetics, high cyclic capacity, and a long history of pilot- and plant-scale use.  Its thermodynamic representation remains challenging because absorbed \COtwo does not behave as a neutral dissolved gas alone.  Instead, it reacts with water and amine to form a liquid mixture containing molecular species, protonated amine, carbamate, bicarbonate, carbonate, hydronium, and hydroxide.  The equilibrium gas pressure is therefore coupled to both phase equilibrium and chemical speciation.

\subsection{Amine Thermodynamics and MEA Data}
Engineering models for \COtwo-loaded amines have historically relied on apparent-species correlations or activity-coefficient models.  Kent--Eisenberg and related apparent-equilibrium approaches provide compact correlations but have limited transferability outside their fitted temperature, loading, and solvent-composition windows.  Electrolyte-NRTL and related excess-Gibbs-energy models improved the representation of ionic liquid nonideality and are widely used in process simulators for \MEA--\COtwo--\HtwoO systems\cite{Zhang2011,Morgan2017b,Mouhoubi2020,Hilliard2008}.  These models are valuable for process design, but their predictive reliability depends strongly on fitted interaction parameters and on the species convention used for the regression.

The experimental basis for the \MEA system is broad enough to support a more molecularly grounded model.  VLE measurements provide \COtwo partial pressures over ranges of temperature, loading, and amine mass fraction\cite{Jou1995,Hilliard2008,Mamun2005,Xu2011,Aronu2011}.  NMR measurements provide independent liquid-phase speciation constraints for \MEA, \MEAH, \MEACOO, \HCOthree, and carbonate-containing species\cite{Jakobsen2005,Bottinger2008,Matin2012}.  These two data families are complementary.  Pressure data constrain the phase-equilibrium response, whereas speciation data prevent a model from matching pressure through an unphysical liquid composition.

\subsection{SAFT and PC-SAFT Models for Alkanolamines}
SAFT-type equations of state provide an alternative to purely excess-Gibbs-energy descriptions because molecular size, dispersion, chain formation, and association are represented in the Helmholtz-energy model.  The PC-SAFT equation of Gross and Sadowski introduced a perturbation theory for chain molecules\cite{Gross2001}, and its associating extension provides the site-based hydrogen-bonding contribution needed for water and alkanolamines\cite{Gross2002}.  PC-SAFT has been applied directly to aqueous ethanolamine systems and acid-gas solubility, including pure alkanolamine property regression and VLE prediction for aqueous amines\cite{Nasrifar2010}.  Baygi and Pahlavanzadeh applied PC-SAFT to \COtwo solubility in aqueous \MEA, using a chemical-equilibrium step to reconstruct liquid species and a neutral PC-SAFT pressure calculation as the phase-equilibrium model\cite{Baygi2015}.  Other SAFT variants have also been used for loaded MEA, including transferable SAFT-VR models that represent the chemisorption stoichiometry through effective association sites\cite{MacDowell2010}.  More recent SAFT comparisons for alkanolamines continue to show that EOS-based models can describe pure, binary, and ternary amine properties with physically interpretable parameters\cite{Pakravesh2025a}.

These studies establish the relevance of SAFT-family models for amine thermodynamics, but most PC-SAFT treatments of loaded \MEA either use an apparent chemistry representation or handle the reactive liquid composition outside the electrolyte EOS.  Consequently, the liquid nonideality of ionic species is not fully coupled to the reaction equilibria through ePC-SAFT activities.

\subsection{Electrolyte PC-SAFT and Advanced Born Contributions}
Electrolyte PC-SAFT (ePC-SAFT) extends PC-SAFT to charged species by adding an electrolyte contribution to the residual Helmholtz energy.  The original aqueous-electrolyte formulation introduced Debye--Huckel electrostatic effects into the PC-SAFT framework and demonstrated accurate correlations for aqueous salt densities and vapor pressures\cite{Cameretti2005}.  Uyan et al. then applied ePC-SAFT to aqueous MDEA--\COtwo solubility with explicit ionic species and activity-coupled chemical equilibrium, treating the calculation as predictive when no binary parameters were adjusted to the ternary acid-gas data\cite{Uyan2015}.  Wangler et al. extended that amine-solubility strategy by fitting selected binary interactions to independent osmotic-coefficient data and validating the resulting model against acid-gas solubility, thereby establishing the useful distinction between literature-collected pure-component parameters, independently regressed binary parameters, and final VLE validation data\cite{Wangler2018}.  Later ePC-SAFT developments addressed non-aqueous and mixed-solvent electrolytes, where ion solvation and composition-dependent permittivity become central.  The ePC-SAFT advanced formulation of Buelow, Ascani, and Held incorporated a concentration-dependent dielectric constant into both the Born contribution and Debye--Huckel term\cite{Bulow2020}; the second part extended the framework to salt solubility and ion pairing\cite{Bulow2021}.  Subsequent work used ePC-SAFT advanced to predict \COtwo solubility in aqueous and organic electrolyte solutions, including systems with carbonate chemistry\cite{Schick2023}.  More recent studies further refined permittivity modeling and modified Born terms in electrolyte PC-SAFT\cite{Rueben2024,Figiel2025}.

The loaded \MEA system is a natural target for this model class.  It contains associating neutral molecules, molecular \COtwo, strong concentration changes in solvent composition, and multiple ionic products of acid--base chemistry.  A full-ionic ePC-SAFT model can therefore connect phase equilibrium, reaction equilibrium, ionic activity coefficients, and solvation effects in a single thermodynamic framework.

\subsection{Objective and Contributions}
The objective of this work is to construct and evaluate a full-ionic ePC-SAFT description of aqueous \MEA loaded with \COtwo.  The intended contribution is not merely a new empirical curve fit to \COtwo pressure data.  The contribution is a thermodynamic parameterization procedure in which literature VLE and speciation data are expressed on a common basis, the reactive liquid is represented by true species, ePC-SAFT activity coefficients enter the chemical-equilibrium residuals, and a single ePC-SAFT model supplies the liquid nonideality and vapor--liquid-equilibrium fugacity quantities.

This manuscript makes four specific contributions.  First, it assembles and documents the VLE/speciation evidence base for \MEA--\COtwo--\HtwoO, including independent pressure and liquid-composition targets.  Second, it defines a nine-species electrolyte basis suitable for ePC-SAFT with \COtwo, \MEA, \HtwoO, \MEAH, \MEACOO, \HCOthree, \COthree, \HthreeO, and \Hydroxide, and it reports the evidence tier for each promoted parameter.  Third, it performs a real-data regression of the best-supported amine-family ion parameters, \MEAH and \MEACOO, using direct NMR/speciation data rather than accepting initial values as fitted results.  Fourth, it evaluates the resulting full ionic parameter set against the assembled pressure and speciation data using the SSM+DS Born and concentration-dependent dielectric terms in ePC-SAFT.
