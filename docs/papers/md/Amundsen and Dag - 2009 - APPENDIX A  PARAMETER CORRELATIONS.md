\section*{APPENDIX B: PROCESS DEVELOPMENT FACILITY (PDF)}

The PCC Process Development Facility (or PDF for short) is located in Newcastle, Australia at the CSIRO Energy Centre. Its purpose is as a test facility for solvent and process improvements at a scale that is useful to provide adequate design information for process scale up. The PDF was designed for a CO2 capacity of $25 \mathrm{~kg} . \mathrm{hr}^{-1}$ and a total gas flow rate of $72 \mathrm{~nm}^{3} . \mathrm{hr}^{-1}$. A simplified flow diagram of the PDF is shown in Figure 1. The complete PID of the PDF is provided in Appendix B.

The facility has the following distinct features:
- Modular design - The plant design is flexible to facilitate installation of additional or alternate equipment with relative ease and it has flexible connections and sufficient space for future process modifications.
- Synthetic gas - A mixture of pure component gases is prepared locally allowing preparation of varying compositions.
- Separate solvent vessels - Two separate solvent tanks allow independent absorber/desorber operation.
- Separate heat exchangers - A wide range of operability in terms of temperature control and vapour liquid fractions is possible.
- Separate washing section - This enables the treatment of both the solvent vapour leaving the absorber and the $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ from the desorber.
- Reflux system - A separate reflux tank allows variation of the reflux condensate return to the desorber.
- Provisions for gas sampling at each stage in the columns - The multiport absorber and desorber columns allow for quantitative gas analysis at points along the column.

\section*{Process Description}

Solvent is prepared by mass in balance mounted solvent tanks, which each have a capacity of 600 litres. Both tanks can be preloaded with $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ or sparged with $\mathrm{N}_{2}$ via a sparger ring located in their bases. Demineralised water is added to the solvent tank via a Continental SDI MB034 demineraliser. Solvent passes through a variable area rotameter via a variable speed solvent feed pump to the top of the absorber. The absorber and desorber can be operated independently or in series in a traditional process configuration.

The feed gas consisting of $\mathrm{N}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ (BOC Australia) can be heated via a feed gas heater and humidified using steam from a steam evaporator. The mass flow rates of $\mathrm{N}_{2}$ and $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ are controlled and measured using Bronkhurst mass flow controllers ( $\mathrm{N}_{2}-1.2-60 \mathrm{~nm}^{3} . \mathrm{hr}^{-1}, \mathrm{CO}_{2}- 0.24-12 \mathrm{~nm}^{3} . \mathrm{hr}^{-1}$ ). The gas is humidified and the temperature and pressure is measured as it is introduced to the bottom of the absorber.

The absorber column has 8 packed sections (each being a flanged pipe spool piece 0.308 m high and 0.148 m diameter) with 1 packed section above the solvent inlet acting as a demister. Each of the packed sections is separated by a sample section (also consisting of a flanged pipe spool piece). The column has a total height of 5.7 meters. The packing used was 16 mm metal Pall rings. The absorber has 10 temperature probes located in ports between the packed sections. The sample sections between the packing also contain gas sampling ports to allow measurement of the gas composition along the column. The liquid level at the base of the absorber is maintained by controlling the pump speeds of the solvent inlet and outlet pumps. CO2 loaded (rich) solvent was sampled after the liquid outlet. The composition of the liquid was determined by acid-base titrations of the samples.

The desorber is similar to the absorber having the same height and same number of packed and sample sections. The outlet gas stream from the desorber passes through a condenser to remove water and its mass flow is measured prior to its release to the atmosphere. The top two sections of packing act as a demister. The rich solvent is introduced below the second packed section while the reflux condensate enters below the first packed section. The level of the reflux condensate was controlled to maintain the water balance in the desorber. Heat in the desorber was provided by the rich solvent heater and the reboiler. A constant circulation rate of the solvent was maintained by controlling the pump speed. Heat in the reboiler was varied using a back pressure control valve which controls the boil up ratio and keeps the reboiler temperature higher than the base of the desorber.

Two different approaches were used to determine the amount of $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ absorbed and desorbed; the liquid flow rate and the $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ and amine content of liquid samples taken at the inlet and the outlet of the absorber and desorber; and the gas flow in conjunction with the gas phase $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ content at the absorber outlet. Determination of the $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ and amine content of liquid samples was by acid-base titration and the $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ content of the gas phase was measured using an NDIR gas analyser. To avoid interference from water the gas flow was dried before passing it to the analyser.

The $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ mass balance was reasonably good with deviations between the figures calculated from the gas and liquid measurements ranging from 0 to $0.76 \mathrm{~kg}^{-1} \mathrm{hr}^{-1}$. The largest discrepancy was for the operating point E with a 17.1\% difference, whereas the average difference was
8.6\%. In terms of CO2 desorbed, the liquid and gas side measurements agree reasonably well with an average deviation of $9.4 \%$.

Typically the liquid analysis indicated a larger amount of $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ absorbed than that determined using the gas analyser, whereas we would expect the contrary as some $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ may be lost by flashing during the sampling process. It was not possible to determine which data was more reliable (liquid or gas) so an average of the two values was used.

\section*{Heat of absorption}

The enthalpy of absorption is a crucial parameter for the heat released during mass transfer and thus the temperature profiles. The absorber model uses a correlation from Perry's Chemical Engineers Handbook (Perry and Green, 1997) to predict the heat of vaporization of water, and this can be considered highly reliable.

The $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ enthalpy of absorption was calculated using the chemical model to calculate the $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ partial pressure as a function of temperature and the Clausius-Clapeyron equation. According to this relationship the slope of a plot of $\ln \mathrm{P}$ versus $\frac{1}{\square \pi}$ yields the enthalpy of absorption.

$$
\begin{equation*}
\ln \mathrm{P}=-\frac{\Delta \mathrm{H}_{\mathrm{CO} 2}}{\mathrm{RT}}+\mathrm{C} \tag{Eq. D 1}
\end{equation*}
$$


The enthalpy predicted via this approach was $\sim 83 \mathrm{~kJ} \mathrm{~mol}^{-1}$ and it was relatively invariant over the range of $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ loadings and temperatures considered. This value is in good agreement with experimental calorimetric measurements (Kim and Svendsen, 2007) as shown in Figure D 1. Thus, an error in the enthalpy of absorption cannot explain the discrepancy.

\section*{Back-mixing and the flow regime}

\section*{APPENDIX E: DETAILS OF DESORBER MODEL}

The desorber has four main steps:
1) A reboiler calculation which uses the calculated heat losses in the reboiler, measured reboiler duty, reboiler flow rate, reboiler temperature, reboiler pressure, pressure at the bottom of the desorber, lean solvent composition (determined by chemical analysis of liquid samples) and outlet liquid flow rate as inputs. The calculation determines the gas flow rate, composition and temperature at the bottom of the desorber, and the liquid composition and flow rate just below the first packed section thanks to a change of state calculation, a flash calculation and a mass balance calculation.
2) A column calculation using the ode15s solver, which goes from the bottom of the first packed section to the solvent inlet. The liquid temperature is initially set to the value indicated by the sensor located just below the first desorber packed section (TI-43). If this temperature results in an invalid model result (evaporation or excessive condensation), it is iteratively changed until a satisfactory result is achieved according to an algorithm detailed in the following sections.
3) An inlet solvent flash calculation assuming that no gas is vaporized in the solvent pre-heater (the validity of this assumption was confirmed by a valve calculation). The column pressure at the solvent inlet is taken from previous column calculation in step 2, the solvent flow rate and pressure from PDF measurements and liquid composition from the chemical analysis of samples. The mass balance and the heat balance at the solvent inlet between the result of the column and flash calculations is then closed by vaporizing liquid or condensing gas with the reflux flow rate as a degree of freedom.
4) A second column calculation using the ode15s solver, which goes from the solvent inlet to the reflux inlet. As the reflux is assumed to be only liquid at the inlet, very little $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ desorption occurs and the chemical model was modified as a consequence for that section. As for the first column calculation of step 2, a recalculation of the initial liquid temperature is required. The initial guess is the temperature indicated by the sensor TI-19.

\section*{Reboiler calculation}

The PDF reboiler process is illustrated by Figure E 2. The flow chart of the reboiler calculation is given in Figure E 3. It starts with a change of state calculation. Heat exchanger steam figures are used to calculate the reboiler duty. An evaluation of the heat losses is performed by comparing steam figures to the outlet valve calculation. The determination of the $\mathrm{C}_{\mathrm{v}}$ of the reboiler outlet valve (CTV-69) indicates
the percentage of liquid vaporized upstream. Heat losses in the reboiler are then calculated to fit this percentage. The heat provided by the reboiler is used for two different processes:
- Heating of the liquid to the reboiler temperature
- Vaporizing a fraction of the liquid

To determine the composition of the gas it was assumed that no water mass transfer occurs after the reboiler heat exchanger. The reboiler temperature and pressure are known from experimental measurements. The calculation is based on the following algorithm:
1) An initial gas flow rate is calculated assuming the flashing gas is pure water.
$y_{\mathrm{H} 2 \mathrm{O}}=1_{v} y_{\mathrm{CO} 2}=0$ Eq. E 1

$$
\begin{equation*}
G_{\text {reboiler }}=\frac{Q_{\text {reboler }}-\left(Q_{\text {rest lesses }}+L_{\text {reboler, in }} C_{\mathrm{pL}},\left(T_{\text {rebolier }}\right)\left(T_{\text {reboler }}-T_{\text {in }}\right)\right)}{-\Delta H_{\text {Hzol }}\left(T_{\text {reboller }}\right)} \tag{Eq. E 2}
\end{equation*}
$$

2) A new liquid composition after vaporization is then calculated, and the new liquid water molar fraction is used in Raoult's law to determine the new water partial pressure at equilibrium. This gives the molar fraction of water in the equilibrium gas, assuming that the remainder is $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/7ea65190-ddf3-4bec-8bab-55fe61abff4d-28.jpg?height=119&width=1292&top_left_y=1720&top_left_x=175)
Eq. E 3

$$
\begin{equation*}
P_{\mathrm{H} 2 \mathrm{O}}^{*}=x_{\mathrm{H} 2 \mathrm{O}} \cdot P_{\mathrm{V}, \mathrm{H} 2 \mathrm{O}}\left(T_{\text {reboiler }}\right), y_{\mathrm{H} 2 \mathrm{O}, \text { new }}=\frac{P_{\mathrm{H} 2 \mathrm{O}}^{*}}{P_{\text {reboliler }}}, y_{\text {CO2,new }}=1-y_{\mathrm{H} 2 \mathrm{O}, \text { new }} \tag{Eq. E 4}
\end{equation*}
$$

3) The $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ enthalpy of absorption is calculated by running the chemical model with the new liquid composition. The vaporization rate is then recalculated taking into account the new gas composition.
![](https://cdn.mathpix.com/cropped/7ea65190-ddf3-4bec-8bab-55fe61abff4d-28.jpg?height=108&width=1223&top_left_y=2371&top_left_x=178)
4) Steps 2-4 are repeated until the calculated $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ molar fraction and gas flow rate reach steady state:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \text { then } G_{\text {reboiler }}=G_{\text {reboiler.new } y} y_{C N>}=y_{C N>\text { mast }} \text { and goto step 2 }
\end{aligned}
$$


The biphasic flow is then passed through a valve and released at the bottom of the desorber. A flash calculation is performed to simulate this section. The outlet pressure is known but not the outlet temperature. As a consequence an extra assumption is required for the calculation - that the final gas downstream of the valve (gas formed in the heat exchanger plus the flashing gas) is in equilibrium with the liquid. That is to say no water and $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ mass transfer is assumed downstream of the valve. The calculation is based on the following algorithm:
1) Firstly a flash calculation is performed assuming that the temperature downstream of the valve is equal to the boiling temperature of pure water at the bottom pressure of the column, and the gas flashing in the valve is pure water:

\section*{Column calculation from bottom to solvent inlet}

The ode15s solver was used to solve the system of differential equations from the bottom of the column (just below the first packed section) to the solvent inlet. Unlike the bvp4c solver used in the absorber calculation, ode15s requires only a set of initial conditions as inputs:
- Initial liquid flow, determined from the reboiler calculation
- Initial gas flow, determined from the reboiler calculation
- Initial liquid composition, determined from the reboiler calculation
- Initial gas composition, determined from the reboiler calculation
- Gas temperature, determined from the reboiler calculation
- Liquid temperature, indicated by the temperature indicator TI-43
- Pressure, indicated by the pressure indicator PI-04

As discussed in the section 6.1.4, the behaviour of the model requires a recalculation of the liquid bottom temperature to find one which avoids water vaporization or excessive condensation occurring in the column. At the end of the first run, if vaporization occurred in the column the liquid temperature was decreased by $0.5^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. If excessive condensation occurred, indicated by a small final gas flow, the initial liquid temperature was increased by $0.5^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$. Another calculation was then performed with the new initial conditions.

As soon as one temperature leading to vaporization and another leading to excessive condensation were found, an average of these two temperatures was taken as the initial liquid temperature. This process was repeated until a satisfactory solution was found. In Table D 5 the calculated bottom liquid temperatures are compared to the measurements from the temperature indicator TI-43, located just below the last packed section.

As for the absorber, the sample sections between each of the packed sections are neglected.

\section*{Inlet solvent flash calculation and closing of heat and mass balance}

At the end of the first column calculation, all the profiles are known up to the solvent inlet. No vaporization in the pre-heater is assumed. This assumption is checked by performing a $\mathrm{C}_{\mathrm{v}}$ valve calculation. In order to determine liquid properties inside the column at the solvent inlet a flash calculation across the solvent inlet valve is required. As this calculation is similar to the flash calculation performed in the reboiler module, it is not detailed here. The results of the flash calculation are then compared to the results of the previous column calculation. An additional calculation is then completed to close the mass and heat balance.

This additional mass and heat balance calculation cannot be avoided as most of the steam is produced at the solvent inlet when the solvent and the flashing gas meet the gas going up the column and the
reflux flow, and not directly when the liquid goes through the valve. Upon entry to the column the phase equilibrium is shifted and a lot of mass/heat transfer occurs. Indeed, the flashing gas formed in the valve is saturated in $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ if equilibrium with the rich solvent is assumed, whereas the gas moving up the column is not. A flow diagram explaining the mass and heat transfer processes is shown by Figure E 4.

First, a $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ mass balance is performed. Additional $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ vaporization after flash at the solvent inlet valve is always needed to close the liquid mass balance.

Two additional heat terms must also be considered to close the heat balance:
- The heat required to desorb $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ and close the mass balance.
- The heat released to cool the liquid coming from the solvent inlet, which has a temperature calculated by the flash calculation, to the final liquid temperature calculated during the first column step calculation.

The heat required to heat the flashing gas was neglected as gas heat capacities and flows were small compared to the liquid. The heat brought by the reflux flow was also neglected as its temperature is unknown at this stage. At the end of the run it was calculated and was always found to be small realtive to the rich solvent flow rate.

To compensate these terms, additional water condenses. Then, a water mass balance is performed and a value for the reflux liquid flow rate after its passage through the packed section above the solvent inlet is calculated. It can be compared to the measurement of the reflux but is expected to be higher as water condensation occurs in the packed section where reflux circulates.

\section*{Column calculation from solvent inlet to reflux inlet}

Finally, the column calculation from solvent inlet to reflux inlet is completed. As the liquid in this section (reflux) is assumed to be pure water, the chemical model was adapted. Almost no $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ mass transfer occurs here, but water continues to condense, heating the liquid.

This section is solved in a similar manner to the first column calculation, using the initial value solver ode15s. As for the first column calculation, an iterative recalculation of the initial liquid temperature is
required in order to find a solution. As the reflux flow rate is very small, no temperature indicators in the column are able to measure its temperature when it reaches the solvent inlet.

The initial conditions used for the first run are as follows:
- Liquid flow rate is the reflux flow rate determined from the heat and mass balance calculation detailed in the last section. It is assumed to be pure water saturated in $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$.
- Initial gas flow and composition as the sum of the flashing gas, gas coming from the column and extra $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ stripping.
- First guess for the liquid temperature and gas temperature is the measurement from TI-19.
- Pressure is assumed to be the final pressure of the first column calculation.

As the temperature indicator below the demister indicates a value which is a value between the gas and reflux temperature, and the gas/liquid temperature difference is quite high at this stage (inlet reflux temperature is around $25^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ ), no temperature measurements are available across the demister. As a result the demister is neglected.

\section*{Behaviour of the model towards initial conditions}

Due to the critical balance between $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ desorption and water condensation the model was found to be very sensitive to the initial conditions and on the initial temperature in particular. The temperature has most influence on the initial $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ and water mass transfer as the total pressure remains quite low in the column and the gas is unsaturated in $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$. A small variation in the initial temperature can lead to either excessive water condensation resulting in zero gas flow or water vaporization.

This behaviour required a refinement of the initial temperature around the experimental value until a value was found that gave a valid solution. It was found that a difference as small as $0.0001^{\circ} \mathrm{C}$ in the initial temperature could lead to vaporization or excessive condensation and a zero gas flow before the top of the column.
